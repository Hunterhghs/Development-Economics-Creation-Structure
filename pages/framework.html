Self-similar scaling tells us whether efficiency gains achieved at one scale are likely to hold when replicated at another.

The Hierarchy of Creation

Development economics is the study of what happens when civilisations fail to climb the hierarchy of creation at the same pace. The diffusion of general-purpose technologies is the mechanism by which the hierarchy re-synchronises across societies. Each GPT is an emergence event: a moment when a new layer of the creation hierarchy becomes accessible to a civilisation that can receive it.

The productivity paradox that follows every major GPT — the lag between visible deployment and measurable output — is the time required for the receiving medium to reorganise itself around the new capability. With electricity, the lag was approximately three decades. With computing, roughly two. With AI, the early indications suggest the lag may compress further.

What this implies for the current moment is clarifying rather than comfortable. The diffusion of artificial intelligence will not produce uniform global productivity gains. It will produce outcomes that are mathematically structured: rapid gains where absorptive capacity is high, stagnation or regression where the medium cannot receive the wave, and threshold-dependent connectivity effects that are easy to underestimate and hard to reverse once missed.

The grammar of spread is universal. The S-curve, the attractor basin, the percolation threshold, the symmetry break — these are not development economics borrowing metaphors from physics. They are physics operating through economies.

~400,000 BCE

Fire & Agriculture

The first reorganisation of human energy systems, compressing the caloric extraction rate from the environment and enabling settled civilisation.

~3,000 BCE

Writing & Mathematics

The externalisation of cognitive capacity, enabling institutional coordination at scales beyond kinship and the accumulation of knowledge across generations.

1760–1840

Mechanical Power (Steam, Electricity)

The substitution of physical labour with captured energy, restructuring production and urban geography. Productivity lag: ~30 years.

1940–2000

Digital Computation & The Internet

The externalisation of information processing, compressing transaction costs and enabling global supply chains. Productivity lag: ~20 years.

2020–Present

Artificial Intelligence

The partial externalisation of cognitive pattern recognition. The ultimate scope — and the diffusion gap — are not yet determined. Productivity lag: compressing.

Implications for Navigation

If this framing holds up, a few practical shifts follow for anyone working on long-horizon development strategy:

01

Treat scaling exponents as diagnostic tools

When a pilot succeeds, the question isn't just "did it work" but "what's the scaling exponent, and does it hold across the geographies we plan to expand into?"

02

Map network topology before designing interventions

Identifying critical nodes — key suppliers, key institutions, key information hubs — matters as much as identifying target populations.

03

Watch for volatility, not just averages

Rising variance and cross-indicator correlation may signal proximity to a regime shift well before the shift itself is visible in headline numbers.

04

Design for composition, not just connectivity

Given that connectivity drives both diffusion and contagion, the goal should be shaping what flows through existing networks rather than suppressing the networks themselves.

The policy implication is precise. If absorptive capacity is a transmission coefficient, you raise it the same way you raise D in any physical medium: through density, connectivity, and energy input. Institutions are the substrate. Infrastructure is the medium. Technology is the wave. The equation is already written. The question is whether the medium is ready to receive it.
